## The Problem PPO Solves

Vanilla policy gradient methods can fail catastrophically: a single large update can push the policy so far from where it was that it becomes worse, and subsequent updates make it even worse — a cycle that's hard to recover from.

<ConceptCheck
  question="In PPO, the importance ratio r_t = π_θ(a|s) / π_θ_old(a|s) is computed. If r_t = 2.0 and the advantage A_t = +3.0, what does PPO's clipped objective do, and why?"
  answer="Without clipping: the objective would be r_t × A_t = 2.0 × 3.0 = 6.0 — a large update that increases this action's probability a lot. With ε=0.2 clipping: clip(2.0, 0.8, 1.2) = 1.2. The clipped value: 1.2 × 3.0 = 3.6. PPO takes the minimum: min(6.0, 3.6) = 3.6. The update is capped. Why: r_t = 2.0 means the new policy is already twice as likely to take this action as the old policy was. Even though the action was good, we've already made a big change — capping prevents further runaway increase that could destabilize training."
