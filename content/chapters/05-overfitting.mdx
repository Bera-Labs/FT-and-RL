## The Generalization Problem

Every ML model is trained on a finite dataset. The goal is not to perform well on that dataset specifically — it's to perform well on **new data** the model has never seen. This ability is called **generalization**.

There are three possible outcomes when you train a model:

## Bias-Variance Tradeoff

The tension between underfitting and overfitting has a formal name: the **bias-variance tradeoff**.

<MermaidDiagram chart={`graph LR
  classDef high fill:#fee2e2,stroke:#f87171,color:#7f1d1d
  classDef low fill:#dcfce7,stroke:#4ade80,color:#14532d
  classDef mid fill:#ede9fe,stroke:#8b5cf6,color:#4c1d95

  SIMPLE["Simple model\n(few parameters)\n────────────────\nHigh bias\n→ misses real patterns\n→ underfitting"] --> SWEET["✅ Sweet spot\nbalanced complexity\n────────────────\nLow bias + low variance\n→ generalizes well"]
  SWEET --> COMPLEX["Complex model\n(many parameters)\n────────────────\nHigh variance\n→ memorizes noise\n→ overfitting"]

  class SIMPLE high
  class SWEET low
  class COMPLEX high`}
/>

- **Bias**: error from wrong assumptions — model too simple to capture the pattern
- **Variance**: error from sensitivity to noise — model memorizes random quirks of training data
- You can't eliminate both at once by just tweaking the model. You need more data, better regularization, or a better-suited architecture.

## Summary: Your Anti-Overfitting Checklist

| Technique | When to use | Strength |
|---|---|---|
| Train/val/test split | Always | Prevents test leakage |
| Loss curve monitoring | Always | Early warning system |
| Early stopping | Most cases | Free, no downside |
| L2 regularization (weight decay) | Most cases | Especially useful for large models |
| Dropout | Deep networks | Computationally free ensemble |
| Data augmentation | When data is limited | High impact, domain-specific |
