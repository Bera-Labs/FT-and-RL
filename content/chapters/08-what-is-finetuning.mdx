## What is Fine-Tuning?

A pre-trained model (Chapter 6) knows a lot — grammar, facts, reasoning patterns, writing styles. But it was trained to predict text in general. Your application needs something more specific: a customer service chatbot, a medical report summarizer, a code reviewer, a sentiment classifier.

Fine-tuning is the process of **continuing training** a pre-trained model on a smaller, task-specific dataset so it learns to excel at your particular job.

## Fine-Tuning vs. Few-Shot Prompting

Before fine-tuning, ask: can you solve the problem with better prompting?

  P["Few-shot prompting\n──────────────────\n✓ Zero setup cost\n✓ Easy to change\n✓ No GPU needed\n✗ Inconsistent outputs\n✗ Context window limits\n✗ Expensive at scale\n(pay per token)"]
  F["Fine-tuning\n──────────────────\n✓ Consistent outputs\n✓ No context window cost\n✓ Cheap inference at scale\n✓ Can teach new styles/domains\n✗ Needs labeled data\n✗ Upfront training cost\n✗ Slower to iterate"]

  class P prompt
  class F ft`}
/>

Use few-shot prompting first. Fine-tune when you need consistency, want to bake in domain knowledge, or need cost-efficient inference at scale.

## When Fine-Tuning Isn't Enough: Domain-Adaptive Pre-Training

Fine-tuning excels at behavioral change — output format, tone, task specialization. But sometimes the model lacks the underlying knowledge to perform your task well, because your domain was underrepresented in the original pre-training corpus.

**Domain-Adaptive Pre-Training (DAPT)** bridges this gap. Before fine-tuning, you run next-token prediction on a large unlabeled corpus from your domain — medical papers, legal documents, financial filings, niche code repositories. This builds the domain's vocabulary, concepts, and reasoning patterns into the model's weights. Fine-tuning then has a much stronger foundation to work from.

**When to use DAPT:**
- Your domain has specialized vocabulary the base model rarely saw (radiology reports, legal Latin, assembly code)
- Task-specific fine-tuning gives poor results even with good data and hyperparameters
- You have access to a large unlabeled domain corpus (millions of documents)

**When to skip DAPT:**
- Your domain uses general language (customer service, marketing copy, general Q&A)
- You have limited compute — DAPT requires the same infrastructure as pre-training
- A domain-adapted base model already exists (BioBERT for bio, CodeLlama for code)
