## A Different Approach to RL

Q-learning asks: *what is the value of each action?* Then derives a policy from that.

Policy gradient methods ask: *what parameters make this policy produce more reward?* And optimize directly.

<ConceptCheck
  question="REINFORCE assigns a return of G=+8 to every action taken in an episode that ended with a win. But action 3 was actually a mistake that the agent recovered from. How does the baseline subtraction help correct this?"
  answer="Without a baseline: every action gets updated positively by +8, including the mistake at step 3. With a baseline V(s): if the expected value at step 3 was +10 (the agent was in a strong position), the advantage is 8−10 = −2. That action gets pushed DOWN even though the overall episode was positive. The baseline captures 'what should have happened given the state' — if the actual return was lower than the baseline at step 3, that action was likely suboptimal, and the policy is correctly nudged away from it. This is why advantage = G − V is much more informative than raw return G."
/>
